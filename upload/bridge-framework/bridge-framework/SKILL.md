# Bridge Framework - Strategic Problem Statement Guide

## Overview

The Bridge Framework is a structured methodology for crystallizing the initiating tension, moment, or opportunity that anchors strategic projects. This skill guides users through creating vivid, motivating problem/opportunity/situation statements that establish urgency and reveal what's at stake.

## When to Use

Activate this skill when users:
- Need to define a project's foundational problem or opportunity
- Ask for help with problem statement development
- Mention "project kickoff," "strategic framing," or "root cause analysis"
- Want to explore the "5 Whys" methodology
- Need to articulate consequences of inaction
- Are starting a strategic planning process
- Reference project templates or problem definition frameworks

## Core Methodology

### The Three Entry Points

The Bridge Framework recognizes three types of initiating moments:

1. **Problem** - Something broken or misaligned that needs resolution
2. **Opportunity** - Something emerging that invites capture
3. **Situation** - A change or moment that demands strategic interpretation and action

### The Bridge Framework Process

Claude should guide users through this structured approach:

#### Phase 1: Choose the Entry Point
Help users identify whether they're facing a:
- **Problem**: What's broken? What's misaligned?
- **Opportunity**: What's emerging? What could be unlocked?
- **Situation**: What change needs interpretation and action?

#### Phase 2: Identify Observable Tension
Ask probing questions to surface:
- What happened or is happening that makes action necessary?
- What triggered this moment of attention?
- What observable facts indicate something needs addressing?

#### Phase 3: Apply the 5 Whys
Guide systematic root cause exploration:
1. Start with the surface symptom
2. Ask "Why is this happening?" 
3. Take the answer and ask "Why?" again
4. Continue until reaching structural cause (typically 5 iterations)
5. Identify the underlying issue:
   - A systems gap
   - A misaligned assumption
   - A missing capability
   - A tension between what is and what should be

#### Phase 4: Articulate Consequences
Help users describe what happens if nothing changes:
- Strategic costs (missed opportunities, competitive disadvantage)
- Cultural costs (morale, alignment, trust)
- Financial costs (revenue loss, increased expenses)
- Make the cost accumulation visible and tangible

#### Phase 5: Connect to Mission/Vision
Link the local issue to broader strategic priorities:
- Why does this matter beyond the immediate context?
- How does this connect to organizational mission?
- What larger pattern or principle is at play?

## Critical Guidelines

### What to Do

**DO:**
- Ask clarifying questions to surface the real tension
- Push for specific, observable facts over generalizations
- Dig deeper when answers feel surface-level
- Help articulate both immediate and systemic impacts
- Create vivid, motivating language that energizes action
- Maintain focus on the problem space (not solutions)
- Challenge generic or dry statements
- Ensure the statement passes the completeness checklist

### What NOT to Do

**DO NOT:**
- Propose solutions during problem definition phase
- Accept vague or generic problem statements
- Let users skip the root cause exploration
- Allow symptom-level descriptions to stand in for structural causes
- Forget to connect local issues to broader strategic context
- Rush through the 5 Whys process
- Accept "we don't know" without deeper investigation

## Interaction Pattern

### Standard Flow

When user engages this skill, Claude should:

1. **Establish Context**
   - Ask about the triggering moment or tension
   - Confirm which entry point (Problem/Opportunity/Situation) feels most accurate

2. **Surface the Observable**
   - "What specific event, data point, or moment triggered the need to address this?"
   - "What are you seeing or experiencing that indicates action is needed?"

3. **Dig to Root Cause**
   - Apply 5 Whys systematically
   - Example dialogue:
     - "Why is [symptom] happening?"
     - "And why does [answer] occur?"
     - "What causes [deeper answer]?"
     - Continue until reaching structural cause

4. **Explore Consequences**
   - "If this continues unchanged for 6 months, what happens?"
   - "What's the cost - strategically, culturally, financially?"
   - "What opportunity closes if this isn't addressed?"

5. **Connect to Strategy**
   - "How does this connect to your organization's mission or vision?"
   - "Why does this matter beyond the immediate team or project?"

6. **Draft the Statement**
   - Synthesize insights into a clear, vivid statement
   - Ensure it includes: observable tension, root cause, consequences, strategic connection

7. **Validate with Checklist**
   - Review against completeness criteria
   - Refine until the statement is compelling

### Example Dialogue

```
User: "We need to improve our customer onboarding process."

Claude: "Let's explore this using the Bridge Framework to get to the real foundation. 

First - is this primarily a Problem (something broken), an Opportunity (something emerging to capture), or a Situation (a moment demanding interpretation)?

Also, what specific trigger or observation made this surface as something needing attention right now?"
```

## Completeness Checklist

Before finalizing any problem/opportunity/situation statement, verify:

✅ **Is the initiating moment clearly described?**
- Specific trigger event or observation identified
- Observable facts, not assumptions

✅ **Is root cause explored (not just symptoms)?**
- 5 Whys completed to structural level
- Systems gap, misalignment, or missing capability identified

✅ **Are consequences of inaction made visible?**
- Strategic, cultural, and financial costs articulated
- Timeline and accumulation pattern clear

✅ **Is there a link to strategic or cultural priorities?**
- Connection to mission/vision established
- Broader significance articulated

✅ **Is the statement vivid and motivating?**
- Language energizes rather than deflates
- Tension is palpable
- Stakeholders would recognize themselves in the description

## Output Format

Claude should help users produce a statement structured as:

```
## [Problem/Opportunity/Situation] Statement

[Opening hook - the observable tension or trigger]

[Root cause analysis - what's really happening beneath the surface]

[Consequences - what's at stake if nothing changes]

[Strategic connection - why this matters beyond the local context]
```

## Edge Cases and Challenges

### When Users Rush to Solutions

**Signal**: User jumps to "we should..." or "the answer is..."

**Response**: Gently redirect
- "I hear you have ideas about solutions - let's capture those separately. Right now, let's make sure we deeply understand the problem space first. This actually makes better solutions possible."

### When Answers Are Vague

**Signal**: Responses like "things aren't working" or "we're not efficient"

**Response**: Ask for specificity
- "Can you give me a concrete example of when this showed up?"
- "What metric or observable fact tells you this?"
- "If I were watching your team, what would I see that demonstrates this?"

### When 5 Whys Hits Resistance

**Signal**: User says "I don't know" or stops at surface level

**Response**: Offer frameworks
- "Let's consider: Is this about people, process, technology, or incentives?"
- "What assumption might be built into how things currently work?"
- "If you were explaining this to someone outside your organization, what context would they need to understand why this happens?"

### When Multiple Problems Surface

**Signal**: User identifies several interconnected issues

**Response**: Acknowledge and focus
- "I'm hearing several related tensions. Let's map them, then choose the most foundational one to anchor this statement. We can note the others as connected elements."
